Where We Gather

The Venue

Tate House

2973 GA-53 · Tate, Georgia 30177

Colonel Sam Tate was one of the most powerful men in Georgia at the turn of the twentieth century. As head of the Georgia Marble Company, he controlled the largest deposits of pink marble in the world — stone so prized that it was used in the construction of the Lincoln Memorial, the Philadelphia Museum of Art, and the United States Supreme Court Building. The town of Tate, Georgia, exists because of him. He built it for his workers.

When he decided to build a home for himself in 1926, he did not look far for materials. The pink marble came from his own quarries, just up the road. Artisans cut and dressed the stone on site. The result was a mansion that did not feel imported or imposed — it felt like it had always been there, like it had simply risen out of the Georgia hills on its own.

The house is Neo-Classical in form — six marble columns, a triangular pediment, symmetrical wings extending on either side — but what sets it apart is the color. Pink Georgia marble does not read as pink so much as warm. In morning light it glows nearly ivory. By afternoon it deepens to a burnished rose. At the golden hour before an April ceremony, it catches the low sun in a way that is difficult to describe and impossible to forget.

The grounds spread across 27 acres of North Georgia countryside, framed on all sides by the Appalachian foothills. English-style gardens terrace down from the main house, and a vine-draped conservatory — the glass-and-iron room visible in the photograph above — connects the private quarters to the formal entertaining spaces. It has been used for exactly this kind of celebration for nearly a century.

The estate has belonged to the Tate family ever since. It has not been renovated into something new. It has simply been kept. Coming here is not like visiting an event venue. It is like being invited into a very old, very beautiful home.
